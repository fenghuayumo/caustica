path tracing, addressing the prohibitive computational overhead inherent in
driving high-density 3D displays. While offering immersive glasses-free 3D
experiences, these displays necessitate the synthesis of massive angular in-
formation across numerous viewpoints to satisfy their optical requirements.

spatial-angular mapping, resulting in redundant computations and extreme
ray counts that preclude interactive performance. We resolve this by refor-
mulating light field synthesis as a sparse signal reconstruction task on a
high-dimensional manifold. We introduce a unified algebraic framework gov-
erned by three mathematical primitives that respectively decouple rendering
logic from heterogeneous optical hardware, establish deterministic geomet-
ric connectivity across disparate viewpoints, and enable atomic access to
unstructured sparse data. Leveraging this formalism, we propose a closed-
loop adaptive rendering pipeline. By integrating adaptive disparity-guided

However, applying real-time path tracing to LFDs remains a formi-
dable challenge [9, 10, 47, 58]. The rendering complexity of a light
field scales linearly with the number of viewpoints required to main-
tain seamless motion parallax. For modern displays necessitating
dozens or hundreds of discrete perspectives to suppress aliasing, this
angular resolution explosion imposes a computational burden that
exceeds the capacity of contemporary real-time rendering pipelines
[22, 27, 32].
This computational barrier is exacerbated by the physical topol-
ogy of automultiscopic hardware, where slanted lenticular arrays in-
troduce lateral spectral offsets that refract sub-pixels into divergent
viewing zones. This architecture invalidates screen-space coher-
ence [19, 66], enforcing a fundamental "3 SPP penalty" for indepen-
dent channel tracing and often necessitating rigid, device-specific
implementations [13, 16]. Crucially, the resulting sub-pixel parallax
interlacing renders existing real-time solutions, which predicate on
2D spatial continuity, inapplicable [3, 43]. Because adjacent display
elements sample disjoint regions of phase space, state-of-the-art
denoisers assuming piecewise smoothness [24, 44, 60] misinterpret
these high-frequency angular discontinuities as noise, leading to
catastrophic ghosting artifacts.
We address these challenges by reformulating light field render-
ing as sparse signal reconstruction on a high-dimensional mani-
fold [5, 26]. Leveraging intrinsic 4D coherence, we propose a unified
algebraic framework governed by three fundamental primitives
designed to abstract optical heterogeneity, establish deterministic
geometric connectivity across viewpoints, and facilitate unstruc-
tured data access. This formalism decouples rendering from device
parameters, enabling a geometry-driven active sensing pipeline [42,
65]. Continuous radiance is subsequently recovered via specialized
spatio-angular integration that explicitly models sub-pixel parallax,
effectively converting hardware constraints into a sparse processing
advantage.
To validate our approach, we implement a high-performance
rendering system built upon a modern Embedded Domain-Specific
Language (EDSL). This architecture enables fine-grained control
over GPU scheduling, achieving interactive frame rates on 8K dis-
plays with 96 viewpoints. Our primary contributions are:
• A unified algebraic framework (Φ,Ψ,Υ ) that decouples light
transport from heterogeneous optical hardware, enabling
rigorous manifold navigation and coherent reconstruction
on unstructured, sparse ray sets.
• A closed-loop adaptive pipeline that integrates disparity-
guided sampling, spatio-angular integration and 5D tempo-
ral accumulation. By modulating a stochastic checkerboard
pattern with local disparity cues, this pipeline achieves high-
fidelity reconstruction at sub-pixel rates without the latency
of feedback loops.
• The first real-time path tracer capable of driving 4K and 8K
automultiscopic displays at interactive rates, with source
code release to facilitate future research.

2.2 Monte Carlo Denoising & Adaptive Sampling
Real-time Monte Carlo reconstruction from sparse samples [65] is
standardly addressed by Spatiotemporal Variance-Guided Filtering
(SVGF) [43, 44] or advanced reservoir resampling strategies like
ReSTIR [3, 39], often paired with rejection heuristics [ 24]. Alter-
native paradigms eschew pure filtering in favor of feature-based
regression [23], recurrent hierarchical feedback [59, 60], or statisti-
cal error reduction [41, 42]. Crucially, these algorithms predicate on
piecewise spatial continuity—an assumption fundamentally violated
by the spatially interlaced angular trajectories of automultiscopic
hardware. Applying standard kernels to this discontinuous domain
induces severe crosstalk. We therefore generalize these reconstruc-
tion principles to the 5D light field manifold, reformulating the
pipeline to strictly adhere to the signal’s intrinsic angular structure
via geometry-driven sampling and integration.
2.3 Sparse Light Field Reconstruction
Reconstructing continuous light fields from sparse samples [21] is
fundamentally governed by Plenoptic Sampling Theory [5]. While
analytical reconstruction via Fourier slice theorems [ 38], sparse
shearlet representations [51], or variational EPI analysis [53] offers
theoretical guarantees, these methods generally lack the compu-
tational efficiency required for real-time applications. To mitigate
the prohibitive cost of dense sampling, Image-Based Rendering
(IBR) strategies exploit geometric correlations to propagate radi-
ance, evolving from seminal plenoptic modeling [4, 35] to layered
depth representations [45, 64]. These principles underpin modern
paradigms including soft 3D reconstruction [40], Multi-Plane Im-
ages (MPI) [62], deep blending [17], and Local Light Field Fusion
(LLFF) [36]. Although recent advances utilize EPI-guided learn-
ing [56] and generalizable neural rendering [1, 49, 52], these methods
typically rely on heavy inference networks that prioritize offline
fidelity over the strict spatiotemporal consistency necessary for
driving physical displays. Our approach leverages the core tenet of
geometric reuse from this lineage but reformulates it specifically
for the automultiscopic manifold to ensure interactive performance.
3 Methodology
We reformulate real-time automultiscopic rendering as sparse signal
reconstruction on the 5D phase space (Ω×Π×𝑇 ). The dimension-
ality gap between the continuous field and the bounded physical
bandwidth (𝑁𝑡𝑜𝑡𝑎𝑙 ) imposes structural sparsity, making dense in-
tegration intractable. We resolve this via an algebraic formalism
defined by three primitives: theRay Transfer Operator ( Φ)for
optical abstraction, theManifold Transport Operator ( Ψ)for
geometric connectivity, and theSparse Gathering Operator ( Υ)
for radiance aggregation. This framework (Sec. 3.1) underpins the
geometry-driven pipeline detailed in Section 3.2.

Ablation Study.We isolate the contribution of our 5D manifold
transport by comparing againstAngular-Adapted SVGF, which
applies angular weights to 2D filtering kernels. As evidenced in
Table 1, this heuristic approach consistently fails to converge in
high-disparity scenarios, yielding higher RMSE and slower infer-
ence times than our method. Notably, our sparse configuration
(𝜌𝑏𝑎𝑠𝑒 = 1/3) outperforms the full-resolution Angular-Adapted
SVGF in both speed and quality. This confirms that high-frequency
lenticular discontinuities cannot be resolved via screen-space filter-
ing alone; explicitManifold Transport ( Ψ)andSparse Gathering
(Υ)are essential for correctly aggregating radiance along physical
light transport paths. The active sensing trade-off is also clearly
delineated: 𝜌𝑏𝑎𝑠𝑒 = 1/3provides a robust "sweet spot" for maximiz-
ing refresh rates, while 𝜌𝑏𝑎𝑠𝑒 = 1prioritizes high-frequency detail
preservation for complex specular transport.
Limitations & Future Work.Despite these gains, several lim-
itations persist. First, the reliance on a single-layer G-buffer for
manifold transport ( Ψ) precludes the accurate reconstruction of
semi-transparency, volumetrics, and multi-layer refraction, as a sin-
gle depth value is insufficient to guide multi-path gathering. Second,
while shear-guided sampling targets disparity edges, sub-pixel mor-
phological features falling below the baseline sampling frequency
(𝜌𝑏𝑎𝑠𝑒 ) may exhibit aliasing or "sparkling" if they possess low dispar-
ity. Third, 5D temporal accumulation depends on accurate motion
vectors; consequently, complex lighting phenomena (e.g., moving
caustics) that decouple from surface motion may exhibit temporal
lag. Finally, extremely rapid angular viewpoint changes can mo-
mentarily outpace history rejection heuristics, causing transient
disocclusion artifacts. Future work will investigate replacing the
analytical Υ operator with a lightweight neural kernel to handle non-
Lambertian transport and integrating gaze-contingent rendering to
further optimize the ray budget for gigapixel-scale displays.
5 Conclusion
We have presented a unified framework for real-time automulti-
scopic rendering that resolves the fundamental bandwidth bottle-
neck of dense multiview generation. By formalizing phase-space
transport via a hardware-agnostic algebraic system, we successfully
reformulate the rendering problem from brute-force accumulation
to sparse manifold reconstruction. Our geometry-driven active sens-
ing pipeline dynamically redistributes the ray budget based on EPI
shear, enabling high-fidelity 5D reconstruction that scales efficiently
to 8K resolutions. Our approach achieves an order-of-magnitude
performance acceleration across both offline and real-time regimes
compared to state-of-the-art baselines, while simultaneously deliv-
ering superior reconstruction quality. This efficiency breakthrough
unlocks immediate utility for downstream applications, ranging
from high-impact commercial digital signage to responsive inter-
active immersive environments. To foster continued innovation in
next-generation immersive display pipelines, we commit to releas-
ing our source code to the research community.
